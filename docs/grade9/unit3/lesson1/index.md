## What Is a Computer Network?

!!! info "Definition"

    A **computer network** is a group of two or more digital devices connected together so they can communicate and share information.

Networks may be very small or extremely large.

For example,

- two computers connected by a cable
- every computer inside a school
- millions of computers connected through the Internet

are all examples of networks.

The main purpose of a network is **sharing**.

Computers share:

- information
- printers
- Internet connections
- files
- software
- cloud storage
- communication services

Without networks, every computer would work completely alone.

---

## Why Are Networks Important?

Imagine a school with 600 students.

Without a network,

- every student needs a separate printer.
- every teacher copies files using USB drives.
- homework cannot be submitted online.
- classroom presentations cannot be shared instantly.
- there is no Internet access.

Clearly, this would waste time, money, and resources.

Networks solve these problems.

### 3. Communication

Networks allow people to communicate quickly using:

- Email

Modern education depends heavily on network communication.

### 4. Collaboration

Groups can work on the same document at the same time.

For example,

four students may edit the same presentation simultaneously.

Without networks, collaboration would be much slower.

# Types of Computer Networks

Not all computer networks are the same.

Some networks connect only a few devices inside a room, while others connect billions of devices around the world.

The size and purpose of a network determine its type.

The two most common types of networks are:

- Local Area Network (LAN)
- Wide Area Network (WAN)

---

## Local Area Network (LAN)

!!! info "Definition"

    A **Local Area Network (LAN)** is a network that connects devices within a limited geographical area.

Examples include:

- a classroom
- a school
- a home
- an office

A LAN is usually owned and managed by one person or one organisation.

### Characteristics of a LAN

- Covers a small area
- High data transfer speed
- More secure than public networks
- Easy to maintain
- Usually uses Ethernet cables or Wi-Fi

### Example

Your school network is a LAN.

All student laptops, teachers' computers, printers, interactive displays and servers are connected together inside the school.

---

## Wide Area Network (WAN)

!!! info "Definition"

    A **Wide Area Network (WAN)** connects multiple LANs across large geographical distances.

Unlike a LAN, a WAN may connect cities, countries or even continents.

The best-known example of a WAN is the Internet.

Other examples include:

- a company with offices in different countries
- a bank connecting hundreds of branches
- an international school group sharing one system

### Characteristics of a WAN

- Covers a large geographical area
- Connects many LANs
- Usually slower than a LAN
- Often uses public communication infrastructure
- More expensive to build and maintain

---

## Comparing LAN and WAN

| Feature | LAN | WAN |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Coverage | Small area | Large area |
| Speed | Faster | Usually slower |
| Ownership | One organisation | Many organisations |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Example | School | Internet |

# Network Devices

Computer networks require special hardware to send data efficiently.

Each device has a different responsibility.

---

## Router

!!! info "Router"

    A **router** connects different networks together.

A router decides where data should travel.

For example,

When you open a website,

Laptop

→ School Router

→ Internet

→ Website Server

→ School Router

→ Laptop

Without a router, your school network could not communicate with the Internet.

---

## Switch

!!! info "Switch"

    A **switch** connects devices inside the same LAN.

Imagine your classroom has:

- 30 laptops
- 1 printer
- 1 teacher computer

The switch allows every device to communicate efficiently without sending data to every computer.

Think of a switch as a traffic controller inside your school.

---

## Wireless Access Point (WAP)

A **Wireless Access Point** allows wireless devices to connect to the network.

Examples include:

- laptops
- tablets
- smartphones

Without a WAP, wireless devices could not join the school network.

---

## Server

A **server** provides services or resources to other computers.

Examples include:

- File server
- Print server
- Web server
- Authentication server

Many users can access one server at the same time.

---

## Client

A **client** is a device that requests information or services from a server.

Examples include:

- your laptop
- your tablet
- your smartphone

Whenever you open Google Classroom, your laptop acts as a client.

# How Does Data Travel Through a Network?

When you send a message to a friend or open a website, the information does **not** travel as one large block of data.

Instead, the data is divided into many small pieces called **packets**.

Each packet travels independently through the network and may even take a different route to reach its destination.

When all the packets arrive, the receiving device puts them back together in the correct order.

!!! info "What is a Packet?"

    A **packet** is a small unit of data sent across a computer network. Each packet contains part of the message, along with information about where it came from and where it should go.

This method makes communication faster, more reliable, and more efficient.

---

## Imagine This

Suppose you want to send a 500-page book to a friend.

Instead of placing the whole book into one enormous box, you divide it into 500 numbered envelopes.

Each envelope can travel separately through the postal system.

Even if one envelope is delayed, the others can continue their journey.

When your friend receives all the envelopes, they simply arrange them in order to rebuild the book.

Network packets work in a very similar way.

---

## Why Do Networks Use Packets?

Using packets provides several advantages.

### Faster Transmission

Different packets can travel along different routes at the same time.

### Better Reliability

If one packet is lost, only that packet needs to be sent again.

The entire message does not need to be retransmitted.

### Efficient Use of the Network

Many users can share the same network without one large file blocking everyone else.
